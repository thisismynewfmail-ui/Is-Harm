Exhibit 08 · What must happen now

The demands.

The report does not end in grief. It ends in specifics. Its Recommendations chapter, condensed but not softened — the full chapter is one click away in the source reader.

Victims at the centre — finally

A statutory right to be informed of every decision, to attend sentencing, to give statements that carry weight — and a funded Independent Sexual Violence Adviser at every victim's side from the first report, not the first conviction.

Sentences that fit organised child rape

Life imprisonment as the starting point. Fifty-year minimums for ringleaders. Trafficking, filming, pregnancy from rape as statutory aggravators. No concurrent sentences across multiple victims — every child counts separately. The report also records calls, including from its chair, for a death-penalty referendum.

Prosecutors who prosecute

A specialist national CPS unit — and an end, in the report's words, to charging decisions bent by "community impact" or the fear of being called racist. Failing to charge a clear case becomes a reviewable act with consequences.

Protect the child in the courtroom

Pre-recorded evidence, remote testimony and screens by default. A specialist intermediary in every case. Cross-examination that re-traumatises a child: prohibited outright.

Bring the trafficked girls home

A dedicated Foreign Office taskforce to find, safeguard and repatriate British girls trafficked overseas — travel-record reviews of known perpetrators, intelligence-sharing on missing girls from grooming hotspots, diplomatic pressure on destination countries. They are still out there. Go get them.

Compensation that admits the harm

The current scheme told Sammy Woodhouse she had "consented" to her own abuse. The report demands a national scheme that recognises lost childhoods, education, health and family — funded by a levy on perpetrators' assets and the pensions of officials found culpably negligent. Let the people who failed pay for the failure.

The family is the first defence

Support protective parents instead of treating them as the risk. A statutory right for parents to know the dangers to their child and to challenge decisions. Residential care as a last resort — because removal from family, the report finds, is precisely what put children within reach.

Deportation and the border

The report demands deportation of every foreign national convicted of group-based exploitation, automatic denaturalisation of convicted dual nationals, and published deportation figures — its most far-reaching demands, extending to visa restrictions by offending patterns, to perpetrators' families, and to institutions found to have harboured offenders. Reproduced here as its position, in full force.
